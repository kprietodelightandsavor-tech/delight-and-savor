What we believe

A Different Kind of Literary Education

Literature is the main dish — not a side. Every feast has a centerpiece, and for us it is the text: the reading, the thinking, the conversation it opens. From that center, everything else grows — writing, grammar, philosophy, rhetoric, the habits of mind that serve a student in any field they choose.

I

Narration First, Always

Before analysis comes comprehension, and before comprehension comes attention. Charlotte Mason knew this. We build on that foundation — narration is not a warm-up exercise, it is the work. From it, everything else grows naturally.

II

Writing Grown from the Text

Grammar and composition are not separate subjects. We draw 2–3 concrete writing principles each term directly from what we are reading — practicing them in narrations, building toward a literary analysis piece. Craft is learned in context, not in a vacuum.

III

The Great Conversation

We bring Aristotle, Augustine, Rousseau, and other great thinkers into the room. Not as separate lessons, but as voices already present in the literature. Students learn to recognize ideas that resonate across all of human history — and to add their own voice to that conversation.

IV

Direction Without Busywork

So much of what passes for rigor is fill-in-the-blank work with no real connection to the text. We offer concrete structure — enough guidance that you never feel lost — while preserving the freedom to make it work in your own home. Challenged and confident, not overwhelmed.

V

Ideas That Resonate

The point of literature is not to perform analysis — it is to encounter ideas big enough to matter. We want students to leave a unit having genuinely wrestled with something: love, ambition, justice, beauty. These are the things that form a person, not just prepare them for a test.

VI

Habits for Any Future

As Charlotte Mason said, the habits we cultivate are the point. Close reading, careful thinking, articulate expression — these serve a student in science, law, medicine, art, or anywhere they go. A living literature course is not just English class. It is preparation for a life of the mind.

“Finally — something I can use for my high school student. Enough guidance that I’m not lost, enough freedom that it still feels like ours.”

Meet your guide

Curriculum made by a homeschool mama who lives the life.

I’m Kim — founder of Delight & Savor, Charlotte Mason educator, and the woman who teaches co-op literature while managing 46 animals on our 15-acre Texas ranch.

I spent nine years in the public school classroom — teaching middle school literature and language arts, then 11th and 12th grade on-level and AP. I taught AP Language & Rhetoric and AP British Literature, and contributed to curriculum writing for my district. I loved that work. But something was always humming underneath it, a song I couldn’t quite name.

“In the surface of the song one could begin to hear a private melody — repeating itself again and again.”
— John Steinbeck, The Pearl

When I began homeschooling my children five years ago and discovered Charlotte Mason’s philosophy of education, I finally heard the notes. It was the song I’d always been trying to write — ideas before information, narration as the foundation of language, beauty as a legitimate aim of education. It had always been there. I just hadn’t had a name for it yet.

For two years now I’ve been teaching high school literature in our co-op, and it’s there that my curriculum writing has truly come to life. I write to fill the gaps — components I wished existed, materials that trust students with real ideas, guides that honor both the homeschool mama and the serious student. There are wonderful curricula out there, and I use them too. Delight & Savor was born from what I still needed.

Every unit is tested in my own co-op classroom before it reaches yours.

M.A. Literary Studies 9 Years Teaching Charlotte Mason Texas Ranch Life
